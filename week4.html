Week 4 of 5

John 1

Life means more; it is Loving.
True love requires proximity — the Creator enters the creation.

Last week we marveled at God's generosity in sharing His royal authority with fragile, insignificant humans. This week, we see its ultimate expression: the generous God doesn't just share power from a distance — He enters the darkness Himself, becoming flesh to bring light and love from the inside.

Bible Project: Visual Commentary

John 1 — Approximately 10 minutes

Part 1

Reacting to the Video

Take a few minutes to discuss your initial thoughts on the video.

01

Echoes of the Beginning: The video immediately draws a parallel between the opening of Genesis ("In the beginning, God created...") and the opening of John ("In the beginning was the Word..."). How does connecting Jesus' arrival to the original creation of the cosmos change the way you view His mission on earth?

02

Tabernacling Among Us: The commentators highlight that the phrase "made his dwelling" literally means Jesus "pitched his tent" or "tabernacled" among us. How does this image of God moving into our messy neighborhood contrast with the way other religions or cultures view the divine?

03

The Father's Lap: The video ends with the intimate image of Jesus being in the "lap" or "bosom" of the Father, and therefore being the only one who can truly make Him known. If God's fundamental posture is this kind of intimate love, how does that challenge the ways we often try to earn His approval?

Part 2

Diving Deeper into the Text

Let's read a few specific segments of John 1 to see how the Creator's light enters the darkness.

📖 Read John 1:1–5
01

John describes the Word as being both with God and being God, and states that in Him was "life," and that life was the "light of all mankind." Why are the concepts of life and light so tightly woven together here? How have you experienced Christ's light bringing life into a dark or chaotic situation?

02

Verse 5 says, "The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it." If God's love is an unconquerable light, how should that affect our anxiety or despair when looking at the brokenness of the modern world?

📖 Read John 1:9–14
03

John writes that the world was made through Him, yet the world did not recognize Him. What are some ways that we, even as people who attend church, sometimes fail to recognize or receive Jesus when He shows up in our daily lives?

04

Verse 14 is the hinge of the entire passage: "The Word became flesh." True love requires proximity and vulnerability; God didn't just shout instructions from heaven, He became a fragile human. If a "loving" life mimics the incarnation, how can we intentionally move closer to the pain, messiness, and vulnerability of the people around us?

Part 3

Unpacking the Literary Design

John's prologue is a brilliantly crafted piece of ancient poetry. Let's look at how its structure supports its theology.

01

The Greek Logos: John uses the Greek word Logos (translated as "Word") to describe Jesus. To Jewish readers, the "Word" was the power God used to create the universe in Genesis 1. To Greek readers, Logos was the invisible, underlying reason and logic holding the universe together. Why was choosing this specific word such a brilliant, bridging literary move by John to reach a diverse audience?

02

The Chiasm of the Prologue: Scholars note that John 1:1–18 is structured as a chiasm (a staircase of ideas that steps up to a central point and then steps back down). The very center of this poetic structure is verses 12–13: the invitation to become "children of God." Why do you think John placed our adoption into God's family at the structural peak of a poem about the cosmic creation?

03

Repeated Motifs: John repeatedly hammers on a few key words: Life, Light, Witness, Believe, and World. Read back through the passage. How does the constant, rhythmic drumbeat of these specific words set the theological agenda for the rest of John's Gospel?

Part 4

Ian's AI Ruminations

As a math professor and AI enthusiast, I can't help but see echoes of these themes in the technology we build. Let's explore some connections between the ancient reality of the incarnation and the frontiers of modern technology.

01

Code vs. Flesh: In software development, we write code (which shares linguistic roots with the concept of logos or logic) to execute commands in a digital space. But in John 1, God's ultimate code doesn't just run a program; it becomes flesh and bleeds. As we build increasingly advanced AI, what are the fundamental limits of a disembodied intelligence? Can an entity without a vulnerable, physical body ever truly understand or enact "love"?

02

Simulated Empathy: Large Language Models can be prompted to speak with profound empathy, kindness, and understanding. But this is a simulated pattern, not a shared experience of human suffering. If Jesus had remained in heaven and only sent down perfectly empathetic "messages," how would that have fallen short of the incarnational love described in John 1?

03

The Data of God: John says that nobody has ever seen God, but the Word "has made him known." Essentially, Jesus is the perfect, uncorrupted data set revealing the Father's character. When we train AI models, human bias and error always creep into the training data. How does the flawless "translation" of God into human form in Jesus provide an anchor of truth in an era where digital truth is becoming increasingly subjective and manipulated?
